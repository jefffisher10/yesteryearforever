CENTER

Home

The part underneath the rest.

Family is the center of my life.

My wife and daughters matter more to me than any machine, project, question, or system I have ever built. The archive, the essays, the late-night tinkering, the long runs, the telescopes, the music, the journals — all of it exists inside a larger life made of love, responsibility, memory, grief, celebration, and continuity.

I come from a large, close family. We get together often. We celebrate loudly and regularly. Cousins, aunts, uncles, friends, daughters, siblings — the web is large, messy, durable, and alive. My father's death changed that world, but not its importance.

If this site lasts for years, I want it to preserve more than technical artifacts or old posts. I want it to preserve a person in context: what mattered to me, what I paid attention to, what I tried to build, and the people I was lucky enough to love while doing it.

LAYER 1

Surface

The visible shape of the life.

Electronics. Repair. Music. Tinkering. Writing. Nature. Astronomy. Systems. Problem solving. Building things that work. Building things that reveal something. Building things for the simple joy of making them exist. Writing belongs near the center of that list. It is one of the main ways I slow down enough to hear what I actually think.

I grew up on a farm and learned early that life is physical, practical, and full of things that break. Trucks, tractors, welding, wiring, gardens, repairs, roofing, plumbing, framing, cutting, carrying, improvising. That world taught me work ethic, mechanical intuition, adaptability, and a respect for the difference between theory and reality.

Later, music became another major language. So did writing. I played in bands, wrote and recorded albums, opened for national acts, ran sound, and built a recording studio from a gutted building. I also kept returning to essays, journals, and reflective writing as a way to sort through scattered thoughts and make them more honest, precise, and coherent. Around and beneath all of that was a constant attraction to science, technology, nature, and the feeling that the world is far stranger and more beautiful than it first appears.

I identify most strongly with electronics, tinkering, curiosity, and making — more than with any one title.

LAYER 2

Systems

How I tend to move through problems and projects.

Understand the system.
Trace the signal.
Find the fault line.
Repair what is broken.
Improve what can be improved.
Document the pattern.
Repeat.

I tend to learn by building, fixing, modifying, taking apart, testing, and observing. The quickest way for me to understand something has almost always been to get my hands on it. That might mean reading a schematic, writing a script, soldering a board, adjusting a process, laying out a workspace, or building an instrument from scratch just to see what it can teach me.

This systems instinct shows up in everything from electronics repair and diagnostic work, to self-hosted web infrastructure, robotics, weather instrumentation, process design, archival systems, and personal routines like journaling and long-distance running.

Even when a project looks artistic on the surface, there is usually a systems layer beneath it: acoustics inside the recording studio, timing inside the stop-motion video, atmosphere and layout inside the website, structure and process inside writing, repetition and adaptation inside endurance.

LAYER 3

Pattern

How I think about identity, continuity, and structure.

One of the deepest ideas in my life has been the thought that identity may be less about fixed substance and more about continuity, arrangement, and pattern. Not things as static objects, but things as structures persisting through change.

That idea helps explain a lot of what I am drawn to: systems, emergence, old machines, handwritten journals, diagrams, maps, modular design, archives, astronomy, questions about consciousness, and the long fascination with what remains the same when everything else changes.

Looking back, the early signs were obvious: Lego for construction, nature cross-sections on the wall, fascination with inner layers and hidden mechanisms, a love of diagrams and anything that revealed what was going on beneath the visible surface.

It probably also explains why I care so much about records: journals kept for decades, typewriters refurbished and used deliberately, websites designed to preserve thought and memory, projects documented not just because they happened, but because I want some trace of the pattern to remain.

LAYER 4

Reality

The bigger questions underneath the projects.

I am drawn to the tension between perception and reality: what the world feels like from the inside, and what science reveals when you look deeper. Matter and identity. Mechanism and wonder. Consciousness and pattern. What is real. What is felt. What survives explanation. What becomes more mysterious once explained correctly.

Astronomy has always been one path into that feeling. So has the ocean. So has nature. So has electronics. Vastness. Hidden layers. Signal buried inside noise. The sense that there is always more underneath.

I wrote a long essay years ago trying to synthesize some of this: identity as pattern, the inadequacy of ordinary perception, the continuity of matter and life, and what it might mean to live honestly inside a scientific view of reality. Since then, some nuances have shifted, especially around emotion, meaning, and faith, but the core questions remain.

Science has never taken wonder away from me. It has usually intensified it. The real thing is almost always stranger than the simplified version.

LAYER 5

Experience

What keeps this from becoming only abstraction.

Honesty, humility, rationality, truthfulness, and integrity matter deeply to me. But feeling matters too. Maybe more than a strict rationalist version of myself once wanted to admit.

One of the truths I have come to trust is that emotions are not a lesser reality just because they are subjective. They may be the most immediate and human reality we have. People may forget the exact words, but they often remember how they felt in your presence. That matters to me in music, family, friendship, work, writing, and even how I think about religion now.

I have lived with anxiety, long bouts of depression, and what is almost certainly ADHD. Those things are not the whole story, but they are part of the terrain. Over time I have built ways of coping: journaling, exercise, projects, solitude, routine, making, reflection, and the discipline of continuing anyway.

Running has been one of the clearest examples of that. I have run dozens of marathons alone, not as public races and not for medals, but for the inward truth of the thing — the quiet endurance, the private continuity, the unglamorous accumulation of miles.

Writing has been one of the clearest forms of that. Left to itself, my mind can be scattered and fast. Writing gives it shape. It helps me slow down enough to articulate what I feel, what I mean, and what I am actually trying to say.

Music has been another. Handwriting another. Typewriters another. These are all ways of slowing down enough to feel my own life while I am living it.

SELECTED BUILDS

Artifacts

A few things I have built, explored, written, or brought into the world.

Backyard astronomical observatory

astronomy / construction / imaging / scale

I designed and built a professional-grade observatory in my backyard and used it to image galaxies, nebulae, comets, and supernovae. It was a technical structure, but also a philosophical one — a way to make cosmic scale intimate and literal, to stand in the backyard and reach into deep time.

Weather station + simple radio telescope

environmental sensing / data logging / invisible signals

I built a weather tracking system that recorded atmospheric pressure, temperature, and light levels and uploaded them to the internet automatically. Later I folded a simple radio telescope into the setup, extending the project from local atmosphere to the broader idea of detecting what cannot be seen directly.

Recording studio from a gutted building

music / acoustics / design / long-form creation

I built a full recording studio from the ground up in a stripped-down building and used it to write, record, engineer, and produce original music — my own and others'. It was a build that merged construction, sound, atmosphere, electronics, patience, and emotional intent into one place.

Albums, bands, and live performance

songwriting / guitar / radio / venues / emotional architecture

I played in bands for years, wrote and recorded albums, played major venues in Ohio with national acts, and was interviewed on local radio. Music taught me something no machine ever could: that technical craft and emotional truth can coexist, and that sometimes making people feel something matters more than winning an argument.

Robots, rovers, rockets, and homemade machines

robotics / motion / experimentation / play

I have built everything from autonomous junkbots made from discarded parts to remote-controlled rovers with cameras and environmental sensors, along with rockets carrying electronic payloads. These were not commercial products. They were acts of curiosity — ways to learn by making motion, signal, and mechanism tangible.

Printed telescope + custom-modified 3D printer

fabrication / optics / iteration / tools making tools

After modifying a 3D printer for my own purposes, I used it to print a fully functional reflecting telescope and many other objects. I have always loved projects where one tool becomes the means of creating the next: recursive making, capability building capability.

Universal hard drive wiping system

electronics / workflow design / scripting / real-world utility

During my years in electronics service, I designed and implemented a universal hard drive wiping solution involving custom hardware stations, software, scripts, and process design. It solved a practical problem at scale and, to my knowledge, is still in use today.

Electronics repair, diagnostics, and engineering work

schematics / troubleshooting / root cause / signal tracing

Over roughly fifteen years in the electronics industry, I moved from solder technician to repair technician, lead, and then engineering-related roles involving diagnostics, X-ray inspection, BGA work, flying probes, oscilloscopes, chip programmers, server repair, process design, training, documentation, and root-cause analysis. That whole era shaped how I think.

Self-hosted website and archive system

plain HTML / writing / preservation / autonomy

This site is one of the more personal builds. Plain HTML. Lightweight structure. Python automation. An intentionally archival feel. Less portfolio than time capsule, lab notebook, curiosity map, and durable record of half-finished dreams, essays, experiments, and old signals I wanted to preserve.

Stop-motion music video

patience / craft / visual storytelling / obsession

I created a stop-motion music video that took years to complete. It was painstaking, impractical, and exactly the kind of thing I love — the sort of project where the process is part meditation, part obsession, part craftsmanship, and the finished artifact contains the time that was poured into it.

Writing, essays, journals, and typewriters

reflection / posterity / analog texture / continuity

I have kept daily handwritten journals for decades. I refurbish and use antique typewriters. I write essays trying to synthesize questions about reality, identity, consciousness, and meaning. Writing helps me make my own mind legible to myself. It is one of the tools I use to turn noise into shape. I do not always feel as skilled at it as I want to be, and I rarely have as much time for it as I wish, but I keep returning to it anyway. These may be quieter artifacts than a telescope or a robot, but they may be the most important ones.

Business-building and adaptable systems work

operations / infrastructure / problem solving / many hats

In a family-owned seafood business, I helped build online systems, inventory processes, HR infrastructure, branding, IT support, operational scaffolding, and countless practical details required to help a small company grow. It was a different kind of build than electronics, but still very much a systems project.

Clinical engineering

recent chapter / maintenance / human consequence

More recently, I moved into clinical engineering, where I repair and maintain medical equipment. It is a newer chapter, so it does not define me as deeply as music, electronics, tinkering, or the broader builder instinct, but it does bring together technical work, troubleshooting, and direct human consequence in a meaningful way.

TIMELINE
